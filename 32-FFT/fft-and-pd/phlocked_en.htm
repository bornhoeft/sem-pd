9. Phase-locked Vocoder


When a signal is multiplicated by a Hinning-Window a frequency is present in more than one channel. In general the frequency-peak is spread over four channels, only if a frequency is a base frequency it covers three channels, where the amplitude in the middle is twice as big as the neighbours. [2], [4]. For the resynthesis all values > 0 are involved, and because phase changes in adjacent channels are not the same, there appear crosstalks of the phases.

By and by this error is getting bigger, and amplitude-modulations occur, noticeable in the so called "reverberance" of the phase vocoder. 

So you come to the point, where you want to itroduce phase locking of adjacent channels. (This is all taken from Miller [2]). It is based on the assumption to calculate the phase change not only from one channel, but also from the two adjacent channels. Because phases of adjacent channels are (ideally) 180° out of phase, you use the negatives of them. The phase of a sum of complex numbers is mainly effected by the strongest amplitude. So the adjacent channels adept their phase to the one of the main-component. The result sounds clearer than without phase locking.

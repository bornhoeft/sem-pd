5. Convolution


One of the most frequent applications in the frequency domain is the Convolution. If you tried to calculate it in the time domain, you would need a multiple of arithmetic operations. In the frequency domain it is simply a multiplication... This phenomenon appears also conversly: a multiplication in the time domain causes a convolution in the frequency domain, which takes influence among other things on the use of Hanning(and other)-Windows. A consequence is that each frequency-peak broadens by the windowing, which can have advantages, but also disadvantages, read "Effects of Hanning-Windows".

For an understanding of the Convolution of two signal you can think of the reverberation of one signal by another. A large hall, like in a church, influences every sound you produce, in fact at every time, at every pitch and frequency range. It applies a kind of fade out tail to the signal. Technically you can look at the reverb as a hall-sample, doing the following to all frequencies: in the beginning loud, and then gradully fading out, which is - white noise shaped by a simple envelope.

Wanting to calculate this mathematically, you would have one sample after the other to multiplicate with each sample of the hallsignal/hallsample and finally add all particular signals. 

The same effect is reached by a multiplication of the corresponding Frequencies in the frequency domain. You merely have to pay attention to the correct handling of the complex numbers.

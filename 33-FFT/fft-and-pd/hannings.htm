6. Effects of Hanning-Windows


In most cases you will multiplicate a signal, which should be observed with a Hanning-Window. So you give it a fade in and a fade out. As discussed in the chapter "Convolution", the result of the analysis is modified by this multiplication. Frequencies, exactly equal to a basefrequency, appear without windowing as a single sample, with the usage of the Hanning-Window the peak falls into three channels. With frequencies lying between base frequencies, you can see "tails" on the side without windowing, but these tails are reduced by the Hanning-Window. (Fig.17 and 18).
 


Fig. 17
Result of a FFT without Hanning-Window. The left frequency is a base frequency, the right lies between two base frequencies. 		Fig. 18
FFT of the same signal, now with Hanning-Window. The peak of the left frequency falls into three channels, the right one lost his tails.

It is not fully obvious to me in some cases, why it is really necessary to use a Hanning-Window. Some calculations could be done without windowing. But in the majority of cases two overlapping FFTs are used, the second of them starting exactly at half of the period of the first. The Hanning Window manages the crossfading of the two FFTs. The timing of the second calculation-process is done automatically in Pd by the object "block~" when given the value 2 as second argument.
